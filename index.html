About Aeiral Spraying

Timber is possible the largest industry for Oregon, producing around 3.9 million feet of wooden board in 2017. Just like any other produce, timber has been heavily controlled to maximize yield and production. A common practice to increasing yield is the use of herbicides to prevent other plants and bugs from affecting production. Most of Oregon's timber covers vast areas, that tend to be in rough terrain or are far from large cities. This leads to aerial spraying being the cheapest and most effective way for the timber industy to manage pesticides. The main issue the we bring up is that some of the aeiral spraying is being conducted in State Forests. The intended use of State Forests is for education, recreation, and wildlife habitat, which is what they are protected for. However the aerial spraying from the timber organizations is posing a risk to these State Forests. The map above shows the extent of State Forests in Oregon, the location of Oregon's primary waterways, and the aerial sprayings occuring in them. The biggest issue with aeiral spraying is that the chemicals can easily be spread by the wind, in turn this means that the effected areas have the potential to be larger than the recorded, and can effect towns that are near the cites.

The Chemicals

While the chemicals are designed to be non/slighly toxic, tests have proven that many of the chemicals can be harmful to certain groups, including humans. The large biome that is Oregon's State Forets is quite complex, so the full and true impact that the chemcials have on wildlife cannot be guaranteed.
